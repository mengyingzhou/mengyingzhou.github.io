Guests Information

Yanjie Bian

Professor of Sociology, University of Minnesota
Dean of Humanities & Social Sciences; Director, Institute for Empirical Social Science Research, Xi’an Jiaotong University

Professor Bian has made foundational contributions to the study of guanxi networks. Beyond his earlier works on the efficacies of strong ties in China and Singapore, his recent publications on the prevalence and rising significance of guanxi in China’s reform era include: “Corporate Social Capital in Guanxi Culture” (with Lei Zhang, 2014, Research in the Sociology of Organizations); “Information and Favoritism: The Network Effect on Wage Income in China” (with Xianbi Huang and Lei Zhang, 2015, Social Networks); “Beyond the Strength of Social Ties” (with Xianbi Huang, 2015, American Behavioral Scientist); and “The Guanxi Influence on Occupational Attainment in Urban China” (with Xianbi Huang, 2015, Chinese Journal of Sociology).

Bian studied in the 1981 Nankai University Sociology Class (taught by Peter Blau, Fei Xiaotong, Nan Lin, among others) and received his Ph.D. in Sociology from SUNY Albany. He previously served as Chair Professor, Head, and Associate Dean of Social Science and Founding Director of the Survey Research Center at the Hong Kong University of Science and Technology. He co-founded the Chinese General Social Survey and serves on the board of the International Network for Social Network Analysis. Author of 11 books and 100+ articles, his current projects include the sociology of guanxi, a panel study on networks and jobs in Chinese cities, and East Asian social networks. He was recognized as one of Elsevier’s most-cited Chinese researchers in social science in 2014 and 2015.

Ronald S. Burt

Hobart W. Williams Professor of Sociology & Strategy
Booth School of Business, University of Chicago

Professor Burt’s research shows how social networks create advantage, with applications to personal networks and market structure. His books include Structural Holes: The Social Structure of Competition (1992), Brokerage and Closure: An Introduction to Social Capital (2005), and Neighbor Networks: Competitive Advantage Local and Personal (2010, AOM George R. Terry Book Award). Research and teaching materials: faculty site.

After earning his Ph.D. in Sociology from the University of Chicago, he held faculty positions at UC Berkeley, SUNY Albany, and Columbia University before returning to Chicago in 1993. He has also served at INSEAD as Shell Professor of Human Resources and as Vice President of Strategic Learning at Raytheon.

Yunsong Chen

Associate Professor of Sociology, Nanjing University
Deputy Chief, Xuanwu District Government, Nanjing

Supported by the Clarendon Scholarship, Dr. Chen earned his D.Phil. in Sociology at Nuffield College, University of Oxford (2012). His interests span social networks and social relations, including counterfactual causal effects in labor markets across institutional contexts, using big data to connect macro socio-economic indicators with cultural phenomena, and studies on subjective wellbeing and social status in China. His work appears in Social Networks, Social Science Research, British Journal of Sociology, and leading Chinese journals. Honors include the Distinguished Scholarly Publication Award (Lu Xueyi Sociology Development Foundation) and the Fei Hsiao-Tung Scholarship for Distinguished Teachers (2015).

Recent projects extend to historical and contemporary China, such as reconstructing networks among ten thousand Tang and Song poets to assess how social ties affected career and literary success, and computational studies using large-scale digitized data (e.g., Google Books N-gram, major news corpora, and social media) on topics from markets and emotion to suicide reporting and global city visibility.

James A. Evans

Associate Professor; Director, Knowledge Lab
Department of Sociology, University of Chicago

Evans studies the collective system of thinking and knowing—from attention and intuition, the origin of ideas, and shared habits of reasoning to processes of agreement/dispute, accumulation of certainty/doubt, and the texture (novelty, ambiguity, topology) of understanding. He is especially interested in how social and technical institutions (Internet, markets, collaborations) shape innovation and discovery. He builds novel “observatories” via crowdsourcing, text/image extraction, and distributed sensors, and employs machine learning, generative modeling, and social/semantic networks to scale interpretive and field methods and to propose alternatives to current discovery regimes. His research is supported by NSF, NIH, Templeton and others and has appeared in Science, American Journal of Sociology, Social Studies of Science, Administrative Science Quarterly, PLOS Computational Biology, among others, with coverage in Nature, The Economist, Atlantic Monthly, Wired, NPR, BBC, El País, and CNN.

Xiaoming Fu

Professor, Institute of Computer Science
University of Göttingen

Xiaoming Fu received his Ph.D. in Computer Science from Tsinghua University (2000), then served with TU Berlin before joining the University of Göttingen (2002), where he has headed the Computer Networks Group since 2007. He is an IEEE Senior Member and Distinguished Lecturer, and has held leadership roles in IEEE ComSoc and the Internet Technical Committee. His interests include Internet-based systems, applications, and social networks. He has published 150+ peer-reviewed papers, co-edited Social Network Analysis: Interdisciplinary Approaches and Case Studies (CRC, 2016), and coordinated EU projects including GreenICN, CleanSky, and MobileCloud. Awards include ACM ICN 2014 and IEEE LANMAN 2013 Best Paper. More information: profile.

Hong Huang

Ph.D. Candidate
Institute of Computer Science, University of Göttingen

Hong Huang received her M.E. in Electronic Engineering from Tsinghua University (2012) and has been a visiting student at the University of Chicago, Tsinghua University, Fudan University, and Nanjing University, with internships at NEC Labs and SAP. Her research focuses on social network analysis, social influence, and data mining—especially triadic structures, triadic closure, and interaction dynamics—developing mathematical models to predict these patterns.

Nan Lin

Oscar L. Tang Family Professor of Sociology, Emeritus
Department of Sociology, Duke University

Professor Lin (born in China) graduated from Tunghai University (Taiwan) and received his Ph.D. from Michigan State University. He taught at Johns Hopkins and SUNY Albany before joining Duke University as Professor of Sociology and Director of the Asian Pacific Institute. His primary interests include social capital, social networks, economic sociology, social stratification, social support and coping, and China. Over three decades he developed an integrated approach to social capital—spanning theory (e.g., Social Capital: A Theory of Social Structure and Action, Cambridge 2001), measurement (the position generator; Social Capital: An International Research Program, Oxford 2008), and comparative research (Social Capital and Its Institutional Contingency, Routledge 2014). He edited Social Capital: Critical Concepts in Social Sciences (Routledge 2011, four volumes). Honors include Academician at Academia Sinica, the 2010 IACMR Distinguished Research Contribution Award, and service as ASA Vice President (1999–2000). Current projects: capitalism in China; networks embedding the economy; development of homeowners’ associations in China.

Jar-Der Luo

Professor of Sociology; Chairman, Tsinghua Social Network Research Center
Tsinghua University

Luo Jar-Der is president of the Chinese Network for Social Network Studies and researches social capital, trust, big-data social network analysis, self-organization, and Chinese indigenous management (e.g., guanxi and guanxi circles). He organized CNSNS in 2005 and founded Tsinghua’s Community Revitalization Research Center to pilot sustainable revitalization models that integrate public–private partnership, scientific planning, balanced development, step-by-step implementation, and community self-reliance.

Toby E. Stuart

Helzel Chair in Entrepreneurship, Strategy & Innovation; Faculty Director, Lester Center for Entrepreneurship;
Associate Dean (External Affairs); Chair, Management & Organizations
Haas School of Business, University of California, Berkeley

Formerly: Charles Edward Wilson Professor, Harvard Business School; Arthur J. Samberg Professor & Faculty Director, Columbia Business School; Fred G. Steingraber–A.T. Kearney Professor, University of Chicago. Ph.D., Stanford GSB; A.B. (summa cum laude), Carleton College. Recipient of the 2007 Kauffman Prize Medal for Distinguished Research in Entrepreneurship; multiple teaching awards (Columbia, Cornell/NY-Tech, Berkeley Haas). His research examines social networks in entrepreneurship, innovation, and strategy, with current projects on email networks, organizational structure and employment outcomes, matching in online networks, technology entrepreneurship, and collaboration in academic life sciences. Editorial roles include Department Editor, Management Science, and board member, Strategic Management Journal.

Yunjie (Calvin) Xu

Professor, Department of Information Management & Information Systems
School of Management, Fudan University

Dr. Xu earned his Ph.D. in Management Information Systems from Syracuse University and previously served at the National University of Singapore. His research covers e-commerce and knowledge management, focusing on information-seeking behavior (human–search engine interactions; offline knowledge seeking in organizations and teams) and on web design and online marketing. He applies social network analysis in several projects.

Recent interests include precision online advertising leveraging both product and individual social networks, with grants from China Mobile, China Telecom, and UnionPay. His publications appear in Information Systems Research, Journal of Management Information Systems, Journal of the Association for Information Systems, JASIST, and others; he serves on multiple editorial boards.

Christopher B. Yenkey

Assistant Professor of Organizations & Markets
University of Chicago Booth School of Business

Professor Yenkey extends theories of social diversity, social capital, and inter-group trust to market development. His research on Kenya’s Nairobi Securities Exchange analyzes how ethnic boundaries shape information transmission and how coethnicity can paradoxically increase vulnerability to fraud without reducing market trust. Ongoing projects examine how misconduct affects foreign capital flows across African states.

He holds a B.A. in Economics (UT Austin), worked as a macroeconomic forecaster at the Federal Reserve Bank of Kansas City (2001–2003), and earned a Ph.D. in Economic Sociology from Cornell University (2011). He has been a visiting scholar at the Institute for Economic Affairs (Nairobi) and Associate Director of Cornell’s Center for the Study of Economy and Society, with publications in Administrative Science Quarterly and Social Forces and awards including AOM’s William H. Newman Award (2011).
